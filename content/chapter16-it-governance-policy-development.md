# Chapter 16: IT Governance & Policy Development

## Introduction

IT governance and policy development are fundamental to effective IT management and strategic alignment with business objectives. As an Assistant Director IT, understanding these concepts is crucial for establishing proper oversight, ensuring compliance, managing risks, and maximizing the value of IT investments. This chapter covers IT governance frameworks, policy development, service management practices, change management processes, risk management strategies, and financial management of IT resources.

#### Overview
- **Purpose**: Framework for IT governance and management
- **Focus**: Aligning IT with business goals
- **Development**: ISACA (Information Systems Audit and Control Association)
- **Approach**: Process-based framework with enablers
- **Benefits**: Business value delivery, risk management, resource optimization

- **Governance System**: Framework for governance and management
- **Design Factors**: Contextual factors affecting governance

- **Performance Management**: Measuring and monitoring performance

#### COBIT 2019 Domains
- **Evaluate, Direct, and Monitor (EDM)**: Governance domain
- **Align, Plan, and Organize (APO)**: Strategic management
- **Build, Acquire, and Implement (BAI)**: Solution management
- **Deliver, Service, and Support (DSS)**: Service delivery
- **Monitor, Evaluate, and Assess (MEA)**: Performance management

#### Governance and Management Objectives
- **GOV**: Governance of enterprise IT
- **APO**: Align, plan, and organize
- **BAI**: Build, acquire, and implement
- **DSS**: Deliver, service, and support
- **MEA**: Monitor, evaluate, and assess

### ISO 38500: IT Governance Standard

#### Overview
- **Purpose**: Framework for governance of IT in organizations

- **Performance**: IT performance is measured and monitored
- **Conformance**: IT complies with internal and external requirements

#### Application

- **Stakeholder engagement**: Consideration of stakeholder needs

### Risk Identification and Assessment

#### Risk Identification
- **Purpose**: Identify potential risks
- **Methods**: Brainstorming, checklists, expert judgment
- **Scope**: All areas of IT operations
- **Stakeholders**: Involve relevant stakeholders

#### Risk Categories
- **Strategic risks**: Risks to strategic objectives
- **Operational risks**: Risks to operations
- **Financial risks**: Risks to financial performance
- **Compliance risks**: Risks to regulatory compliance
- **Reputational risks**: Risks to reputation

#### Risk Assessment Process
- **Likelihood**: Assess probability of occurrence
- **Impact**: Assess potential impact
- **Risk rating**: Calculate overall risk rating
- **Prioritization**: Prioritize risks for treatment

### Risk Analysis: Qualitative and Quantitative

#### Qualitative Risk Analysis
- **Purpose**: Prioritize risks for further analysis
- **Method**: Probability and impact assessment
- **Output**: Risk register with priority rankings
- **Efficiency**: Efficient use of risk management resources

#### Probability Assessment
- **Scale**: Low, medium, high or numerical scale
- **Basis**: Historical data, expert judgment, analysis
- **Factors**: Past experience, complexity, uncertainty
- **Documentation**: Record probability assessments

#### Impact Assessment
- **Categories**: Cost, schedule, scope, quality, reputation
- **Scale**: Low, medium, high or numerical scale
- **Basis**: Potential effect on business objectives
- **Consideration**: Both positive and negative impacts

#### Quantitative Risk Analysis
- **Purpose**: Numerically analyze effect of risks
- **Method**: Numerical analysis of risk effects
- **Output**: Probability of achieving objectives
- **Application**: Cost and schedule risk analysis

#### Quantitative Methods
- **Monte Carlo simulation**: Analyze impact of uncertainty
- **Decision tree analysis**: Analyze decisions with uncertain outcomes
- **Sensitivity analysis**: Identify variables with greatest impact
- **Expected monetary value**: Calculate expected value of risks

### Risk Mitigation Strategies

#### Risk Treatment Options
- **Avoid**: Eliminate threat entirely
- **Transfer**: Shift risk responsibility to another party
- **Mitigate**: Reduce probability or impact of risk
- **Accept**: Acknowledge risk and take no action

#### Risk Avoidance
- **Purpose**: Eliminate threat entirely
- **Method**: Change project plan to remove risk
- **Application**: High-impact, controllable risks
- **Example**: Change technology to avoid technical risk

#### Risk Transfer
- **Purpose**: Shift risk responsibility to another party
- **Method**: Insurance, contracts, guarantees
- **Application**: Risks that cannot be eliminated
- **Example**: Purchase insurance for equipment damage

#### Risk Mitigation
- **Purpose**: Reduce probability or impact of risk
- **Method**: Take action to decrease risk level
- **Application**: High-impact risks that cannot be avoided
- **Example**: Additional testing to reduce technical risk

#### Risk Acceptance
- **Purpose**: Acknowledge risk and take no action
- **Method**: Passive or active acceptance
- **Application**: Low-impact or cost-prohibitive risks
- **Example**: Accept minor schedule delays

### Risk Monitoring and Review

#### Risk Register Updates
- **Purpose**: Keep risk information current

#### Risk Reviews

## Practice Questions

1. What are the four dimensions of service management in ITIL 4?
2. Explain the difference between an SLA and an OLA.
3. What are the six principles of ISO 38500?
4. Name three types of changes in change management.
5. What does the acronym TCO stand for and what does it include?

## Answers

1. The four dimensions of service management in ITIL 4 are: Organizations and People, Information and Technology, Partners and Suppliers, and Value Streams and Processes.
2. An SLA (Service Level Agreement) is an agreement between a service provider and a customer defining expected service levels, while an OLA (Operational Level Agreement) is an internal agreement between different IT departments supporting SLA achievement.
3. The six principles of ISO 38500 are: Responsibility, Strategy, Acquisition, Performance, Conformance, and Human Behavior.
4. The three types of changes in change management are: Standard changes (pre-authorized, low-risk), Normal changes (follow standard process), and Emergency changes (urgent, expedited process).
5. TCO stands for Total Cost of Ownership, which includes all costs associated with acquiring, operating, maintaining, and disposing of IT assets throughout their lifecycle.
